# Google Design Principles

Use this reference for the judgment layer behind a Google/Material redesign.

## Product Judgment

Google Design treats design as an evolving practice across Material Design, Google Fonts, accessibility, privacy, AI, motion, art direction, and product craft. Apply that breadth by asking what the interface helps people do, not whether it merely resembles a Google product.

Use these principles:

- Make technology feel approachable, useful, and human.
- Start from a real user need and a concrete task.
- Reduce friction before adding expression.
- Preserve user agency: clear choices, visible state, reversibility, and control.
- Make unfamiliar capability feel familiar through known UI patterns.
- Test with realistic content and behavior, not only idealized mock data.
- Let craft serve comprehension: type, motion, color, sound, and haptics should all clarify meaning.

## Human-Centered AI

For AI-enabled frames:

- Anchor the feature in a human need before choosing AI.
- Explain what the system can do, what it cannot do, and what the user can control.
- Prefer augmentation over automation when trust or authorship matters.
- Let people review, correct, undo, and calibrate the system.
- Avoid "magic" UI that hides responsibility, uncertainty, or data use.
- Use familiar patterns when the model behavior is novel or unpredictable.
- Evaluate with real user content when possible; fake models can still validate the UX.

## Accessibility

Accessible design improves the baseline experience for everyone. Do not treat it as a checklist at the end.

When redesigning:

- Research how people with different abilities complete the task today.
- Try assistive technologies when practical, but do not assume that brief simulation equals lived experience.
- Observe real use and ask about workarounds, friction, and context.
- Support screen readers, keyboard use, text resizing, captions/transcripts, low vision, color blindness, motor constraints, and cognitive load.
- Provide non-color cues, human-readable labels, clear structure, and forgiving inputs.

## Motion and Interaction

Motion should make interfaces meaningful:

- Guide attention to what changed.
- Preserve object continuity during navigation and expansion.
- Respond quickly to user input.
- Use easing and duration to feel natural, not theatrical.
- Avoid motion that increases wait, nausea, or uncertainty.
- Provide reduced-motion alternatives.

## Ergonomics

Frame-level ergonomics should account for hands, eyes, memory, and repeated use:

- Keep primary actions reachable on the target device.
- Keep scan paths short and predictable.
- Place controls near the content they affect.
- Use familiar icons with labels when recognition may be weak.
- Reduce typing with defaults, recent choices, chips, steppers, and structured inputs.
- Support one-handed mobile use when plausible.
- Design for interruption: autosave, undo, clear state, and recoverable errors.

## Google-Like Craft Without Imitation

Do:

- Use clean surfaces, direct copy, generous but purposeful spacing, strong hierarchy, and friendly interaction.
- Use Material Symbols and Material 3 components where they fit.
- Borrow Google Design's restraint, clarity, accessibility, and human-centered process.

Do not:

- Copy Google product screens or editorial art direction.
- Overuse playful color, oversized rounded shapes, or animation when the user's task is dense or operational.
- Force Material 3 when a product's existing system already solves the interaction better.
