# Portfolio Framework

## Target Definition

Answer these before designing pages:

- What role is this portfolio trying to win? Examples: Product Designer, UX Designer, UI Designer, Mobile Designer, Senior Product Designer, Design Lead.
- What company type should it fit? Examples: startup, big tech, agency, product company, enterprise, AI product, fintech, consumer app.
- Which 3 skills should stand out? Examples: research, product thinking, UI craft, systems thinking, design systems, facilitation, experimentation, prototyping, growth, mobile interaction.
- What should a reviewer understand about the candidate in 3 minutes?

Turn the answers into one positioning sentence:

`I am a [level/role] who helps [company/product type] achieve [business/user goal] through [3 proof skills].`

## Case-Study Selection

Score each candidate case from 1-3:

- Relevance to target role.
- Clear candidate ownership.
- Clear problem and constraints.
- Strong process and tradeoffs.
- Visible result, learning, or impact.
- Visual quality and artifact availability.

Choose 2-4 cases. Prefer fewer stronger stories over 8-10 shallow entries.

## Portfolio Structure

Homepage:

- Name and role positioning.
- One-sentence value proposition.
- 2-4 featured case studies with clear labels.
- Credibility signals: companies, domains, outcomes, methods, shipped products, awards, teaching, leadership.
- Short about preview.
- Contact link or call to action visible without hunting.

Case-study preview cards:

- Project title.
- Product/domain.
- Role and scope.
- Outcome or strongest proof.
- Skill signal, such as research-led redesign, design system, mobile growth, enterprise workflow, AI feature, or cross-functional launch.

About:

- Keep biography relevant to the hiring target.
- Connect unusual background to design judgment only when it strengthens the story.
- Avoid generic passion statements unless backed by proof.

Contact:

- Include email or clear contact route.
- Make LinkedIn/resume easy to find when useful.

## Special Cases

Student or side projects:

- Present as real projects.
- Include problem framing, constraints, feedback, tests, iterations, and what changed.
- Avoid "I redesigned X because I felt like it" framing.

NDA projects:

- Remove brand names, exact numbers, confidential screens, and identifying customer details.
- Explain context in generic terms.
- Show process diagrams, anonymized flows, before/after abstractions, decision logic, and relative impact.
- One real anonymized NDA case is usually stronger than several unrelated side projects.
